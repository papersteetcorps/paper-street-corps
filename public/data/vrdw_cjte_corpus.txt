1. Terminologies
- Subject: Subject, in Jung’s framework, refers to the conscious center of experience—the standpoint from which perception and judgment occur—meaning the living psychological entity that encounters, interprets, and responds to reality; it is not merely the ego in isolation but the experiencing pole of consciousness that stands in contrast to the object, and while the ego serves as the organizing center of awareness, the subject denotes the position of inward reference from which phenomena are apprehended, so that its essence lies in being the locus of experience, the inner point from which reality is perceived and evaluated.
- Object: Object, in Jung’s framework, refers to that which is experienced by the subject, meaning any content—external or internal—that stands in contrast to the conscious standpoint and is perceived, judged, or related to by it; it may be a physical thing in the outer world, a person, an event, or even an inner image, thought, or emotion when these are treated as contents of awareness, so that its essence lies in being the experienced pole of reality, that which confronts the subject as something given and toward which psychological functions are directed.
- Ego: Ego, in Jung’s framework, is the center of conscious awareness and the organizing principle of personal identity, comprising the thoughts, memories, and perceptions that an individual recognizes as “I,” and functioning as the mediator that selects, integrates, and coordinates experiences within the field of consciousness; it is not the whole psyche but only its conscious core, standing in dynamic relation to the unconscious, so that its essence lies in being the focal point of conscious orientation and self-reference through which the subject experiences and relates to reality.
- Suppressed: Consciously holding back something.
- Repressed: Unconsciously blocked from awareness.

2. Function Groups
- Sensing (S): Sensing, in Jung’s framework, is the psychological function that perceives reality through direct sensory registration, establishing that something exists by transmitting concrete stimuli to consciousness without interpreting, judging, or inferring beyond what is immediately given; it is an irrational function because it does not reason or evaluate but simply receives and affirms actuality—when extraverted it attends to objective, tangible facts and immerses awareness in the physical environment as it stands, and when introverted it focuses on the subjective impression or inner image evoked by the stimulus, yet in both orientations its essence remains the same: it grounds consciousness in the immediate fact of what is present.
- Intuition (N): Intuition, in Jung’s framework, is the psychological function that perceives possibilities, meanings, and latent developments that are not directly given in sensory data; it does not focus on what is concretely present but on what could emerge, what is implied, or what is becoming, grasping patterns, connections, and future outcomes through unconscious perception rather than deliberate reasoning, and as an irrational function it does not judge or analyze but apprehends potentialities directly—when extraverted it tracks possibilities inherent in the external situation and shifts from one emerging prospect to another, and when introverted it perceives inner images, symbolic meanings, and archetypal patterns arising from the unconscious—yet in both orientations its essence remains the same: it apprehends what is not yet actual but is possible.
- Thinking (T): Thinking, in Jung’s framework, is the psychological function that organizes and evaluates perceptions by means of logical connection, determining what something is by forming concepts, making distinctions, and establishing relationships according to objective or subjective principles; it is a rational function because it judges and concludes through reasoning rather than merely perceiving, aiming at coherence, consistency, and structured understanding—when extraverted it aligns its judgments with objective data, external standards, or shared logical systems, and when introverted it builds and refines internally consistent frameworks rooted in subjective principles—yet in both orientations its essence remains the same: it seeks to define, classify, and make sense of reality through logical judgment.
- Feeling (F): Feeling, in Jung’s framework, is the psychological function that evaluates perceptions according to value, determining whether something is acceptable or unacceptable, agreeable or disagreeable, worthy or unworthy, and it is a rational function because it makes judgments—not through logical analysis like thinking, but through a consistent process of valuation that establishes the importance or significance of what is perceived; it is not mere emotion or affect, but a deliberate assessment of value—when extraverted it aligns its valuations with external standards, shared norms, or collective values, and when introverted it refers to an internal, often private and deeply held scale of values—yet in both orientations its essence remains the same: it assigns value and determines the worth of reality.

3. Function Directions
- Introverted functions (i): Introverted functions, in Jung’s framework, are psychological functions whose orientation is directed primarily toward the subjective factor rather than the external object, meaning that perception or judgment is governed by inner images, internal principles, or personal valuations instead of adapting directly to objective conditions; the object is not ignored, but it is filtered through an inwardly determined standpoint that shapes how it is experienced or evaluated, and because the center of gravity lies within the psyche, the resulting perception or judgment may appear detached, reserved, or independent from collective standards—yet in essence, introversion does not change the core nature of a function (sensing still perceives actuality, intuition still perceives possibility, thinking still judges logically, feeling still assigns value), it only determines that the decisive reference point is internal rather than external.
- Extraverted functions (e): Extraverted functions, in Jung’s framework, are psychological functions whose orientation is directed primarily toward the objective factor, meaning that perception or judgment is governed by external conditions, observable facts, shared systems, or collectively recognized values rather than by inwardly derived standards; the inner standpoint is not absent, but it is subordinated to what is given in the environment, so adaptation occurs through alignment with the outer situation, and because the center of gravity lies in the object, the resulting perception or judgment tends to be responsive, situational, and outwardly expressed—yet in essence, extraversion does not alter the fundamental nature of a function (sensing still establishes actuality, intuition still apprehends possibility, thinking still forms logical judgments, feeling still assigns value), it only determines that the decisive reference point is external rather than internal.

3. Cognitive Functions
- Introverted sensing (Si): Introverted sensing, in Jung’s framework, is the perceptive function oriented toward the subjective factor of sensation, meaning it does not focus primarily on the external object itself but on the inner impression, image, or sensory trace that the object evokes within the individual, apprehending reality through how it is internally experienced rather than how it appears objectively; as an irrational function it does not judge or interpret but registers and relives the qualitative imprint of perception, often linking present stimuli to deeply rooted internal images shaped by prior experience or archetypal patterns, so that what is perceived is filtered through the individual’s inner sensory world, yet its essence remains the same as sensation generally: it establishes that something is, but through the medium of subjective impression rather than direct immersion in the outer fact.
- Extraverted sensing (Se): Extraverted sensing, in Jung’s framework, is the perceptive function oriented toward the objective sensory qualities of the external world, apprehending reality by directly registering what is tangibly present—form, color, movement, texture, sound—without interpreting or valuing it, and as an irrational function it does not reason or judge but immerses consciousness in the immediate fact of the object as it stands, adapting fluidly to concrete circumstances and responding to actual conditions rather than abstractions or possibilities, so that its essence remains the straightforward affirmation of what exists in the outer environment here and now.
- Introverted intuition (Ni): Introverted intuition, in Jung’s framework, is the perceptive function oriented toward the inner world of unconscious images and symbolic patterns, apprehending not the external object itself but the underlying meaning, trajectory, or archetypal significance that arises within the psyche, and as an irrational function it does not reason or evaluate but directly perceives possibilities in the form of internal visions, insights, and symbolic impressions that often appear detached from immediate sensory reality; rather than tracking outer developments, it follows the movement of inner images toward their implicit culmination, so that its essence remains the perception of what is becoming, but through the lens of the subjective unconscious rather than the external situation.
- Extraverted intuition (Ne): Extraverted intuition, in Jung’s framework, is the perceptive function oriented toward possibilities emerging from the external situation, apprehending not merely what is present but what could develop from it, and as an irrational function it does not judge or logically derive conclusions but directly perceives latent opportunities, alternative uses, connections, and future outcomes inherent in objects and events; it moves rapidly from one potential to another, energized by unfolding prospects in the environment, so that its essence remains the perception of what is becoming, but grounded in the objective world rather than in inner symbolic imagery.
- Introverted thinking (Ti): Introverted thinking, in Jung’s framework, is the rational judging function oriented toward forming internally coherent principles and conceptual structures derived from subjective foundations rather than external standards, seeking to define, classify, and understand reality through logically consistent frameworks that arise from within; it does not primarily aim to align with collective logic or practical application but to refine ideas until they achieve inner precision and structural integrity, often abstracting from concrete facts to reach essential formulations, so that its essence remains logical judgment, yet governed by an internal criterion of consistency rather than objective consensus.
- Extraverted thinking (Te): Extraverted thinking, in Jung’s framework, is the rational judging function oriented toward organizing reality according to objective logic, external facts, and established systems, determining what something is and how it should be structured by applying principles that are publicly verifiable and practically effective; it seeks order, efficiency, and measurable coherence in the outer world, aligning judgments with observable data and collective standards rather than private conceptual refinement, so that its essence remains logical evaluation, but directed toward shaping and managing objective conditions according to rational criteria.
- Introverted feeling (Fi): Introverted feeling, in Jung’s framework, is the rational judging function oriented toward an internal, often private system of values by which reality is evaluated according to deeply held and individually formed standards of worth, determining whether something is acceptable, meaningful, or authentic not through external norms but through inwardly experienced conviction; it does not necessarily display emotion outwardly, for its valuations may remain concealed and quietly decisive, yet its essence remains the same as feeling generally: it makes judgments of value, governed by a subjective and internally consistent scale rather than collective or socially shared criteria.
- Extraverted feeling (Fe): Extraverted feeling, in Jung’s framework, is the rational judging function oriented toward evaluating reality according to objective or collectively recognized values, determining what is appropriate, acceptable, or harmonious by aligning judgments with shared norms, social expectations, and relational considerations; it seeks to maintain accord between individuals and their environment by adjusting valuations to fit the emotional and ethical atmosphere of the situation, expressing value judgments outwardly and responsively, so that its essence remains the assignment of worth, but guided by external standards and the requirements of interpersonal harmony rather than private conviction.

4. Function Positioning (Dom > Aux > Tert > Inf)
- Dominant function (Dom): The dominant function, in Jung’s framework, is the psychological function that is most differentiated, consciously developed, and reliably under the control of the ego, serving as the primary mode through which an individual orients to reality and adapts to the world; it sets the overall direction of consciousness, shaping perception and judgment according to its nature—whether sensing, intuition, thinking, or feeling—and because it is the most refined and stable function, it tends to suppress or subordinate its opposite, creating a structured imbalance in the psyche, so that its essence remains the central organizing principle of personality, the function upon which consciousness chiefly relies for orientation and decision.
- Auxiliary function (Aux): The auxiliary function, in Jung’s framework, is the secondary psychological function that supports and complements the dominant function by providing balance and breadth to consciousness, being sufficiently differentiated to operate consciously yet not as central or authoritative as the dominant; it serves to assist adaptation by introducing a different mode of orientation—typically opposite in rationality (judging vs. perceiving) but not in attitude—so that the psyche does not become one-sided, and while it does not govern the personality’s overall direction, its essence lies in stabilizing, refining, and extending the effectiveness of the dominant function without displacing its primacy.
- Tertiary function (Tert): The tertiary function, in Jung’s framework, is the less differentiated psychological function that remains partially conscious but comparatively undeveloped and subordinate to both the dominant and auxiliary functions, often expressing itself in a more primitive, inconsistent, or naïve manner; it shares the same attitude (introverted or extraverted) as the dominant function but differs in rationality, and because it lacks full differentiation it does not guide adaptation reliably, instead appearing intermittently in ways that may be playful, compensatory, or immature, so that its essence lies in being a weaker and less integrated mode of orientation within the conscious structure of the personality.
- Inferior function (Inf): The inferior function, in Jung’s framework, is the least differentiated and least conscious psychological function, standing in direct opposition to the dominant function in both type and orientation, and because it is largely undeveloped and repressed, it operates in a primitive, autonomous, and often emotionally charged manner; it is not under reliable ego control and tends to emerge in exaggerated, compulsive, or destabilizing forms, especially under stress, serving as the primary gateway to the unconscious and carrying both the individual’s greatest vulnerability and potential for growth, so that its essence lies in being the weakest conscious function yet the most psychologically significant in terms of depth, tension, and individuation.
